Which Sports Executives Are the Most Politically Active?

By Chilekasi Adele

It’s no secret that many of the nation’s sports owners and executives are multi-billionaires with plenty of capital to spend. Some owners like Hal Steinbrenner, the owner of the New York Yankees, spend lots of their money directly on improving the team. Some, like Jerry Jones of the Dallas Cowboys, look to improve the team, but additionally spend on luxurious items such as yachts, mansions, and marvelous stadiums.

Many, as has been well-documented by multiple people previously, act as pseudo-lobbyists. Often, sports-owners have the communities in which they reside by the horns — threatening to leave and relocate elsewhere if they don’t receive funding for new stadiums.

This was evident recently, when Terry Pegula and his wife Kim, owners of the Buffalo Bills, reportedly sought cities of relocation as they looked to secure local and New York State funding to build their new stadium, replacing the current Highmark Stadium in Orchard Park. A deal was eventually made keeping them in their current Western New York suburb — with a record $850 million in taxpayer funding going towards the new stadium.

America has entered a new age, however. Citizens are now more socially active, and the internet has made it easier for those interested to view the financial activity of the rich and powerful. Owners have made lots of donations in recent years to political campaigns, and some say the reasons why are multifaceted.

We analyzed a set of donations from FiveThirtyEight detailing donations from sports owners in 2016 — the year former President Donald Trump was elected, 2018 — when Democrats took over the House, and 2020 — when current president Joe Biden was elected. Within these three election years, American sports owners and executives donated nearly $47 million to political campaigns.

The highest total amount of money donated was by Charles Johnson, principal owner of Major League Baseball’s San Francisco Giants. Johnson donated over $11 million during the four-year period that the donation data was tracked, making 213 individual donations.

By league, every different league has a different donation leader on top.

WNBA

Kelly Loeffler, former Georgia Senator and former part-owner of the Atlanta Dream led the WNBA in donation amount. Loefller made about $896,000 in donations over the course of the four year period — every single one of her donations going towards Republican or conserative causes. Following her is John Rogers, owner of the Chicago Sky, who donated about $694,000 and Herbert Simon, who donated about $525,000. Simon owns the WNBA’s Indiana Fever, in addition to the NBA’s Indiana Pacers.

MLB

As we mentioned previously, Charles Johnson is the leader in Major League Baseball donations. Johnson made 213 donations — 211 being to Republicans or right-leaning bipartisans, with two donations going to Democrats. Johnson is followed by Peter Angelos, owner of the Baltimore Orioles. Angelos made about $2 million in donations over the four-year period. Following Angelos is Jerry Reinsdorf, the owner of the Chicago White Sox. Reinsdorf made about $1.7 million in donations — 45 of his donations being to Republicans, 27 being to Democratic or mostly democratic campaigns, and three donations to campaigns unaffiliated with a political party. Angelos made two donations to bipartisan groups, and 18 to Democrats.

NASCAR

Roger Penske, owner of Team Penske leads NASCAR owners in donations with about $420,500 over the four-year period. Penske operates the car currently driven by Joey Logano. Penske made 18 total donations — Ten to Democrats, five to Republicans, and three to bipartisan groups. Behind Penske is Rick Hendrick of Hendrick Motorsports ($137,000), and Richard Childress of Richard Childress Racing ($96,100).

NBA

Leading the way in the National Basketball Association is Dan DeVos of the Orlando Magic. DeVos. DeVos’ sister-in-law is Betsy DeVos, former secretary of education under former President Donald Trump. DeVos donated about $2.3 million over the four-year period observed, with all 116 of his donations going to Republicans or right-leaning bipartisans. Jerry Reinsdorf, owner of the Chicago Bulls, and the aforementioned White Sox, is second with nearly $1.8 million in donations. James Dolan is in third with over $1.6 million in donations. Dolan owns the New York Knicks. Dolan made 11 total donations, six of them going to Republicans, and five of them going to Democrats.

NHL

Philip Anschutz, owner of the Los Angeles Kings, leads the National Hockey League in donation amount with nearly $1.8 million. All of Anschultz’s 33 donations are to Republican causes. Following him is the aforementioned James Dolan, who owns the New York Rangers in addition to the NBA’s New York Knicks. Ron Burkle, owner of the Pittsburgh Penguins, follows him with nearly $1.26 million in political donations. Out of Burkle’s 14 total donations, five were to Republicans, while nine were to Democrats.

NFL

The biggest sum in America’s most popular league is that of Jimmy and Susan Haslam, owners of the Cleveland Browns. The Haslams donated over $1.7 million to political causes. Six of their 102 total donations went to bipartisan causes. Two went to bipartisan, but right-leaning causes, and five went to Democrats. A single donation wasn’t identifiable by party — going to the “Cleveland Forward Pac,” and 88 went to Republicans. Janice McNair, owner of the Houston Texans, donated nearly $1.4 million. McNair made 14 donations — 12 of them to Republicans, and two to bipartisan causes. Stephen Ross, owner of the Miami Dolphins, donated nearly $540,000. 50 of Ross’ 65 donations went to GOP causes, with the remaining 15 to Democrats.

WHY DO OWNERS MAKE THE DONATIONS THEY DO?

Professor Rick Burton, an endowed professor of sport management at Syracuse University’s Falk College, says political donations can be used as a “defensive maneuver.” Burton believes public pressure is key to some of these donations, and that the owners, and those who work for them, are watching the social activity of their clientele.

“By showing that I’m in support of a particular cause, I don’t have a blindside,” Burton said. “I can’t be accused of not being supportive. What we really don’t know is whether or not that owner is all in, whether that owner is sincere and walks the walk.”

Arthur Paris, a longtime professor of sociology at Syracuse University’s Maxwell School of Public Affairs, says owners have no problem donating until their activities may come under the spotlight, essentially saying that it’s no problem until it hurts an owner’s bottom line. Paris not only talks about this from a political perspective, but in terms of many day-to-day business issues as well, such as the issue of head trauma and concussions in the NFL.

“As you get closer to a fundamental challenge to their central activity, then I think their resolve hardens,” Paris said.

Burton mentioned that a genuine “passion for the cause” could be behind a specific donation, but there are some business reasons, such as tax breaks and stadium deals.

“If the owner knows that [a cause is] important to a sponsor, that that’s the kind of thing that might cause a sponsor to become a sponsor of the team or to renew a sponsorship,” he said. “It could be because [a cause is] important to an owner’s players…”

In terms of donating to a specific party, Burton says the rhyme and reason is actually quite simple. It’s about influence, and what an owner can get in return for a check.

“[It can be] for the very political reasons of 'Who controls that state’s legislature?'" Burton said. “Who controls big decisions that are going to be made relative to an owner wanting to develop a piece of land — and whether or not that endorsement of a political party or a political individual is going to facilitate their long-term goals?”
